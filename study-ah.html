Study AH

Developer & Designer| March 2020 - May 2020

Study AH is an iOS App that has the full AP Art History curriculum available on annotatable, fact-rich flash cards

WHY?
My old Art History teacher came to me to find a solution to transition his class to remote learning while still preparing for a fast approaching AP Exam
WHO
I handled everything from design to development to publishing on the App Store. I collaborated with Vincent Thearle on gathering content for the app.
HOW
I developed Study AH using Swift and Firebase Tools over a 2 month period. I used tools like Figma, iMovie, and Framer along the way to prototype

Problem
"It is really difficult for me to keep all of the key details about the images in one place to annotate, study, and review"
- AP ART HISTORY STUDENTS

1000's Of Need-To Know Details for the AP Exam

Studying for the AP Art History Exam is very hard. The exam covers 250 images of key artwork, artifacts, and architecture spanning thousands of years of time. For each image you must know a dozen plus details to write succinctly about it on the exam. Unfortunately these details are scattered all around the web and students struggle to organize the images, their details, and the student's notes when its time to study for the AP Exam.

High School Students: Accessibility,Simplification, Distraction-Free

High School Students need class material that is straight to the point, free of distraction or ads, and can be accessible at home, at school, or anywhere that is convenient. It is crucial to consider the technical devide between different school communities and student profiles. Current materials miss this entirely.

Navigating Virtual Learning

Art History Teachers are struggling to adapt their materials to virtual. They are unable to use print outs or posters to teach material that is highly experiential. A solution is needed that can deliver images and their context asynchronously.

Streamlined Flash Cards

USER NEED
It is difficult to associate images to its context and details while revising. Visuals and key information are usually in different places.
SOLUTION
Study AH has flashcards with key dates, mediums, locations, and visuals packed neatly within one entity to make everything you need to know available at a glance.

Easy Annotation

USER NEED
Students find it difficult to connect what they learn in class to the study materials they use. There is often nuance discussed in class that doesn't translate to exam study material.
SOLUTION
Study AH allows users to simply tap on a flashcard and add their notes. This makes Study AH the ultimate classroom tool for students and educators alike.

Available Everywhere

USER NEED
With the shift to remote learning, students need content that is accessible everywhere and they can carry from home to school as conditions change.
SOLUTION
Study AH digitizes hundreds of pages of text book material into an app that can be loaded on to a mobile phone or school tablet
Process

User Research

I interviewed 15 AP Art History students and split them into two groups. I conducted generative user interviews with group one to understand what they wanted to see in a solution. I then conducted attitudinal user interviews with group two to understand what their frustrations were with current solutions. This is how I came to the user needs that informed the solution.

Mapping the User Journey

Once I had a good understanding of what users wanted and the problem at hand, I began to balance what user said they wanted versus what would actually solve their problems. This allowed me to generate a journey map of how users would interact and decide an app was the best form factor for a solution.

Setting Design Priorities
Intentional Choices I Made When Designing Study AH

Unique Identity

To emphasize the interlinking of different content areas, each image belonged to a group that shared similar color hues throughout the interface.

Playful

Since this is an educational product, I wanted to include simple touches, that made the interface playful without being distracting.

Ever Changing

I wanted Study AH to be a fun, dynamic experience. Given content is mostly static, a shifting carousel allows the app to avoid feeling stale.

Prototyping

Study AH is built upon smooth and mission-critical animations. This took a lot of tinkering to get just right. I started in Framer and perfected the transforms and swipe gestures in the iOS simulator.

Developing

I had the pleasure of building Study AH from the ground up for iOS devices using the Swift programming language. Since I had such a solid foundation from Figma and Framer, developing went much smoother.

Making Strategic Product Tradeoffs
Deciding how to integrate tags into Study AH
CONSTRAINTS
The tag view would have to take up a fixed width of the screen (around 30%), but on mobile devices, it would take close to 50%, making both the tags and notes section unusable.
METHEDOLOGY
Any approach to adding Tags had to be uniform to both tablet and mobile. Tags were also much lower on the prioritization matrix than notes were. A decision had to be made to ding notes and keep tags or remove tags.
DECISION
I opted to removing tags and finding a new place for them . Without this decision the notes pane would become crushed vertically on many device sizes. This would go against the "available everywhere" need.

Usability Testing

I conducted a remote unmoderated user testing program with 30 participants through Test Flight and included Hotjar analytics on the app so I could see the heat maps of where users were getting stuck. I  gave users instructions, collected feedback, and did deep dives into qualitative and quantitative data.

Iterating & Publishing

The on-boarding view to the left came about after seeing the heat map of where users were tapping. They seemed to completely miss that the whole card is tappable, so I included onboarding to clarify.

Around May 1st of 2020, I had completed a majority of the usability testing and published on the App Store!

Conclusion

Next Steps

The next steps for Study AH is for me to promote it more on different mediums such as the App Store, build in tagging, and allow the custom creation of content groups based off of tags. I would also consider adding a monetization model into the app.

Learnings Along The Way
I focused on building my prototyping and user research skills
TAKEAWAY #1
You can not take everything the user says they want at face value. Your job as a researcher is to understand their problems and core tasks and then test and iterate ways to solve this problem effectively.
TAKEAWAY #2
User interactions should be mission-critical. Whether it is to make a product more fluid or playful, animations should be purposeful and are best used to support core functionality of the product than a visual gimmick.
TAKEAWAY #3
Use usability testing as a valuable tool during the prototyping stage to refine fundamentals of the product before putting on polish through development. I would slightly change the order of my design process
