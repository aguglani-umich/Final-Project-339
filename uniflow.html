UniFlow Campus

Co-Founder & Designer|December 2019 - Now

WHAT
I manage everything product and design related at UniFlow from the Figma designs to building the product roadmap. I co-founded UniFlow with a few friends back in December of 2019 to centralize all of the events, opportunities, clubs, and departments on a college campus in one place. We provide personalized recommendations of what organizations to get involved in and streamline the process of joining them to provide students with a more holistic college experience. Our campus engagement platform is a web based app that has student, campus leader, and campus administrator portals. It is currently used at 13 colleges and universities around the country including USC, Penn State, and Michigan.

Over 90% of college juniors can look back and think of one student organization they wish they knew about sooner. We want to democratize access and simplify college ecosystems to allow students to find their best-fit communities.

1. Identify Job To Be Done

UniFlow started because my co-founders and I felt extremely lost as freshmen when trying to find our footing on campus and join organizations that brought a sense of community. This was primarily because:

1. We found it very hard to know how to join organizations on our campus
2. Information about extracurricular opportunities was spread over 100's of webpages
3. Existing solutions had a net promoter score of -80% and were nothing more than a link list

When we first sought out to build UniFlow, we wanted to know what the market (students, campus leaders, and campus administrators) really wanted and why existing solutions did not cut it. This process was tricky because students always had the biggest problems, but campus administrators would be our end-customer. Ultimately we knew we wanted to build a software platform and by generating problem statements for each of our stakeholders.

For Students
Students struggle to centralize, understand, and join the best-fit opportunities for them when coming to campus.

For Campus Leaders
Campus Leaders are challenged with communicating the unique value add of their organization and displaying new and relevant information to the student body.

For Campus Administrators
Campus Administrators are flying blind when knowing how engaged their students are. They are also responsible for the frustration of campus leaders and students when it comes to campus engagement.

2. Seek Signal From Users

Listening to what customers care about is one of my highest priorities when building a user experience. I used surveys, questionnaires ,and interviews to conduct primary user research and build personas for key stakeholders when designing the UniFlow platform. I focused in on heuristic evaluation, persona creation, and qualitative research in particular during a 2 month customer discovery process that spanned May to July of 2020.

The basics of the program were to identify user constraints. and gather as much context as possible around how to accomplish the job to be done in the most efficient and effective way as possible. I followed these linear set of steps to gather the signal I needed.

1. I talked to 5 or so friends who studied different subjects at the University of Michigan to get a basic sense of the sheer range of things they would want in a campus engagement / club discovery tool.

2. I created a Google Form using a mix of feature rank choose, short answer ,and this or that questions. I posted this form with completion attached to a lottery for a financial incentive in incoming Freshmen facebook groups at 5 major universities with vastly different profiles (large public, small public, LAC, small-private, large-private). I got around 60 responses per school. This allowed me to create data and charts around student preferences and how they would want the platform to work.

3. I interviewed two people from each school (10 total) who submitted the form and ran through the interview script (excerpt above) to crystallize my hypothesizes. I did these interviews with another colleague who recorded notes while I ran the interview

4. I summarized my findings and compared it against a assumptions grid I created before embarking on the process. I was able to share my findings with the rest of my team and they informed several product decisions in the following months.

These exercise allowed me to pinpoint the genesis of our platform and what value we offered to students. Our signal gathering process for club leaders and administrators was completed by our sales team.

3. Draw, wireframe, & Parallel Prototype

Once I had a broad sense of what users were looking to accomplish and what they value, I started by thinking about product specifications and began to wireframe. I often picked up my iPad and pencil and went to a quiet place to draw out a few different takes on flows that solved user problems. Unfortunately my hand writing is not neat enough to show you a wall of wireframes, but I prioritized getting as rough of a sketch down as possible. This allowed me to take these designs to the team and visualize the product requirements documents I was drafting in parallel. Where needed, I would use Framer to make simplified wireframes and have them be interactive for use in interviews or with the development team.

4. Balance New & Existing Patterns

When building the UniFlow design system, I wanted to combine existing usability heuristics with techniques and approaches unique to our product. I believe this would allows us to create a familiar experience for users, but give us permission to experiment on how we could display information unique to our use case more effectively. When approaching creating a design system, I would go to Dribble and look at my favorite products to see what design patterns I thought were effective and identified how they could apply to the UniFlow platform. I also consulted various brand building principals and color psychology to implement these three existing design techniques to our platform:

Let Unique Content Shine
Highlight unique content from platform stakeholders shine to create fluidity, trust, and compelling visuals

Use Shadows to Depict Hierarchy
Simplify high density information displays by using hierarchy to illuminate important content

White is the New White
Using copious amounts of white allows the interface fade into the background and let dynamic content take center stage. White allows you to deliver a decluttered, luxurious, and bright experience to users.


Through experience in designing older versions of the platform and design books I have read, I decided to bring the following more unique visual patters to our design system.


Build for Mobile First
I used constructs like cards and swipes to reconcile the large amount of Gen-z traffic we have who are used to mobile experiences.

Gradients for UI Elements
Gradients were all the range and then got out of hand to say the least. They have now found their place mostly in product marketing visuals, but I decided to use gradients in our UI elements to be both eye-catching and illuminate the "flow" in UniFlow.

Multiple Data Displays
Everything on UniFlow has two ways to represent it. For example clubs on UniFlow can be viewed as either a popup or a full page. We use two data displays for core constructs on UniFlow to match the correct visual container to the correct situation.

5. Figma + Magic + Developers = Product

Pictured above is our UniFlow Campus Engagement release from January of 2021. I worked on designing the product for over 3 months hand in hand with my CTO. This process included building a product roadmap in ClickUp, organizing tasks in sprints, sizing tasks, creating design release schedules, conducting design reviews, and launching a comprehensive testing and quality assurance program. We would work in two weak sprints, where I would design flows in Figma one sprint before they began development to get feedback from users and customers. Following the conclusion of a sprint, it would be tested, QA'd and go through revisions to make sure it followed specifications and our product requirements documents that I would make. On any given day their would be several processes from drawing PRD's to adjusting the roadmap to getting a sprint's designs ready. This is where the magic came in. As a one person product and design team, I would often enlist sales colleges to help gather signal from the new customers they would talk to or test a particular part of the product. All in all, in everything we did, we kept the user in the center and were able to build a product with a high level of craftsmanship for our customers.

6. Measure, A/B Test, Listen, Repeat

I used agile development cycles to focus on pushing out MVPs and hearing directly from users about how they use our products in their daily life. I split the product into feature families like "Campus Leader - Events" or "Student - Virtual Fairs" to be able to iterate on discrete sections of products and a time. I launched a number of programs such as surveys, user interviews, and A/B tests after each release of a feature family to hear from customers what they liked or didn't like. Through this process, I focused on north stars to help me analyze signal and make product tradeoffs effectively.

An example of the application of our process (pictured above) was our login page. In our August 2020 release, we showed users a generic user splash screen and then took them to a sign in with Microsoft or Google login page. I found through usability testing, students were confused because UniFlow never acknowledged the campus they belonged to during the sign in process. I redesigned the flow to include a school picker to improve the user navigation and success rate of the interaction. Additionally, the new flow was able to improve login speed by knowing which login provider to use based on the users university selection in our initial page which simply did not exists before.
